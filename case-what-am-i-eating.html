Overview

Working files are only a head start if you can actually work with them.

What Am I Eating? is one of the most graphics-dense shows I've worked on — illustrated labels, animated 3D packaging, hand-lettered signs, info cards, stat graphics, character personalities and a full title treatment, all within a single series. Localising it to French came with a package of supplied files, but the project had been built without versioning in mind: inconsistently structured, undocumented, and never intended to be opened by another team. The practical result was the same as having no files at all for most elements.

The job became equal parts localisation, file audit and from-scratch rebuild — with the added challenge that the original fonts weren't supplied, requiring careful matching and substitution throughout.

The brief

[X episodes] of a studio-based food factual series, built around near-constant graphics: illustrated set labels, animated 3D product packaging, handwritten signs, full-screen info cards, stat graphics, animated character sequences and transition elements. Every element needed to exist in French, match the original's look and energy, and turn around on a series schedule.

The supplied package covered most assets — but the structure varied wildly between elements, many files were unlabelled or non-functional, and the original typefaces were not included. The first step was a full audit: what was usable as-is, what could be salvaged, and what needed to be rebuilt from scratch.

The variety problem

Most localisation jobs involve one or two graphic systems. This show had a dozen. Each element type came with its own construction logic, its own illustrated style, and its own text-expansion challenge when moved into French.

The font problem

The original fonts were not supplied with the package. Every typeface across the show — and there were many, given its deliberately varied, hand-crafted aesthetic — had to be identified from reference frames and matched with available alternatives. In some cases a close substitute existed; in others the match required adjusting weight, spacing or size to compensate for visual differences between the original and the replacement.

Where fonts differ from the originals, the rebuilt graphics match the visual intent — weight, scale, colour and layout — rather than the specific letterform. A viewer watching the French version would not notice the substitution.

The text expansion problem

French consistently runs 20–30% longer than English. On a show where text is a visual design element — sized, positioned and animated as part of the illustration — that isn't a find-and-replace. It's a layout redesign for every affected graphic.

3D packaging

Several elements weren't 2D graphics at all — they were text on 3D animated product packaging. Rebuilding these meant matching not just the typography but the perspective, lighting and animation of the original 3D renders, working from flat reference frames.

At scale

Across [X] episodes, the volume of distinct graphic elements — each requiring individual audit, rebuild and localisation — made this one of the most complex localisation jobs in the series run. The variety of illustration styles, font matching challenges, text expansion issues and 3D elements meant almost no two graphics were solved the same way.
